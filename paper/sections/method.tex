\section{Method}

\paragraph{Setup.}
For a question $q$, let $p_\theta(y \mid q)$ denote the model's parametric
belief and let $p_{\mathrm{ctx}}(y \mid q, c)$ denote the answer distribution
induced by retrieved or supplied context $c$. We introduce a latent
context-reliability variable $r \in [0,1]$ and define arbitration as choosing a
predictive distribution that minimizes posterior-predictive risk under a
strictly proper scoring rule.

\paragraph{Theorem 1 target: Bayes-optimal arbitration.}
If context reliability has prior $\pi(r)$ and the answer posterior factorizes
through a reliability-dependent mixture, then the Bayes-optimal predictive
distribution is obtained by integrating over posterior reliability:
\[
p^\star(y \mid q, c) = \int p(y \mid q, c, r)\, p(r \mid q, c)\, dr.
\]
Within the log-linear family used by practical decoders, this becomes a
reliability-aware interpolation
\[
p^\star(y \mid q, c) \propto p_\theta(y \mid q)^{1-w^\star(q,c)}
p_{\mathrm{ctx}}(y \mid q, c)^{w^\star(q,c)},
\]
where $w^\star(q,c)$ is the posterior expectation of context reliability up to
the calibration map induced by the likelihood family. The nontrivial part is
not Bayes' rule itself, but tying $w^\star$ to observable signals such as
retrieval reliability, popularity, dynamicity, contradiction type, and semantic
entropy.

\paragraph{Theorem 2 target: fixed policies are minimax-suboptimal.}
Let $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{fixed}}$ be the class of policies that always trust
context, always trust parametric memory, or use one global mixture weight. If
there exist two conflict families in which context is respectively reliable and
misleading, then every fixed policy incurs $\Omega(1)$ excess Bayes risk on
some mixture over those families, while an adaptive policy that sees a
separating reliability signal can achieve regret proportional to the signal's
classification error. Empirically, this theorem predicts the exact pattern we
measure: fixed trust policies should fail badly not because they are badly
tuned, but because no single global rule can handle both families at once.

\paragraph{Theorem 3 target: two-regime self-correction under conflict.}
Our original theorem-3 target was monotone: under conflict, longer CoT should
strictly worsen calibration. The real-trace evidence rules out that blunt form.
The supported target is now an $\eta$-generalized-Bayes and Berk--Nash style
statement:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Theorem 3'.} Let $p_t(y \mid q, c)$ denote the implicit answer
distribution after $t$ reasoning steps, and model the CoT trace as endogenous
evidence incorporated through a generalized Bayes update with learning rate
$\eta$:
\[
p_{t+1}(y \mid q, c) \propto p_t(y \mid q, c)\,
\exp\!\big(-\eta \, \ell_t(y; q, c, z_t)\big).
\]
Under source conflict and model misspecification, there exists a critical
learning rate $\bar{\eta}(q,c)$ such that for $\eta > \bar{\eta}(q,c)$ the
iterated posterior sharpens toward a wrong KL-projection answer faster than it
improves accuracy. Conflict lowers that threshold, so
$\bar{\eta}(q,c_{\mathrm{conflict}}) < \bar{\eta}(q,c_{\mathrm{no\ conflict}})$.
Empirically, this yields two regimes: naturalistic contradiction can show an
interior overconfidence peak followed by partial recovery, while controlled
conflict at larger scale can remain stuck in a saturated overconfidence regime.
\end{quote}

This is a misspecified-Bayes statement rather than a generic ``thinking more is
bad'' statement. Each reasoning step reweights the relative support of
parametric and contextual explanations, but the evidence is endogenous because
the trace is generated by the same model whose belief is being updated. Under
conflict, posterior sharpening can therefore run ahead of truth-tracking,
producing phantom certainty. Whether the curve later bends back depends on the
effective conflict geometry and on whether the update remains below the
family-specific critical $\eta$ threshold.

\paragraph{Observable proxies and implemented policies.}
The implemented experiments compare six policy families:
\begin{enumerate}
\item `bayes\_proxy': a reliability-aware arbitration rule,
\item `heuristic\_adaptive': a generic non-oracle adaptive baseline,
\item `simulated\_model': the model's own implied arbitration behavior,
\item `fixed\_50',
\item `always\_context',
\item `always\_parametric'.
\end{enumerate}
The first two are the scientifically meaningful comparison. The last three are
the theorem-2 stress tests. The evaluation focuses on regret against an oracle
arbitration distribution, plus accuracy, ECE, Brier score, and the
oracle--model KL gap.
