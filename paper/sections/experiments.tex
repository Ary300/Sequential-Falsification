\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Benchmark and model matrix.}
The current empirical core has two layers. For Theorems 1 and 2, we use a
corrected broad wave spanning PopQA, DynamicQA, NQ-Swap, and WikiContradict,
plus a conflict-heavy wave that emphasizes contradiction and conflict slices.
These waves cover Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Qwen-2.5-14B, Qwen-3-8B,
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, and Pythia-6.9B. For Theorem 3, we use real
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B and 14B generations on WikiContradict and
ambiguity-screened ConflictBank with three reasoning budgets: `no\_cot',
`short\_cot' (`128`), and `long\_cot' (`1024`).

\paragraph{Metrics.}
The arbitration experiments report mean regret against an oracle arbitration
distribution, plus accuracy, ECE, and the oracle--model absolute and KL gaps.
The theorem-3 experiments report accuracy, mean confidence, ECE, and the
confidence--accuracy gap. We emphasize the gap because the core phenomenon is
confidence sharpening without commensurate accuracy gain.

Table~\ref{tab:headline-regret} is the empirical backbone of Theorems 1 and 2.
The Bayes proxy beats the generic adaptive heuristic on both waves, and the gap
widens on the conflict-heavy slice. More importantly, every fixed trust policy
is dramatically worse. This is the practical face of the minimax theorem:
fixed trust is not merely suboptimal, it is operating in the wrong decision
class.

Table~\ref{tab:spotlight-regret} shows that this is not just a small-wave
artifact. On the expanded spotlight-scale benchmark-backed matrix spanning
ConflictBank, FaithEval, MemoTrap, NQ-Swap, and PopQA across five model
families and 174{,}080 parsed examples, the Bayes proxy still beats the generic
heuristic by $0.0833$ regret while fixed trust policies remain catastrophically
bad. The absolute oracle--model arbitration gap stays nontrivial
($0.1932$ mean absolute gap, $1.4680$ mean KL), which means the proxy result is
not vacuous. This expanded matrix is now the strongest theorem-1/theorem-2
evidence in the paper.

The broad wave is also informative about model heterogeneity. Three of the four
main broad-wave models favor the Bayes proxy over the heuristic by about
$0.0319$ regret, but Qwen-2.5-14B-Instruct slightly favors the heuristic
($0.0050$ versus $0.0098$). That exception is scientifically useful. It shows
the theorem-1/2 story is not a tautology baked into the proxy. Likewise, in
the conflict-heavy wave, Pythia-6.9B is essentially tied between the Bayes
proxy and the model-implied policy, while DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B remains
far from the Bayes rule.

\subsection{Theorem 3: Real Reasoning Traces}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Conflict-slice overconfidence gaps on real DeepSeek-R1-Distill traces.}
\label{tab:theorem3}
\begin{tabular}{llccc}
\toprule
Model & Benchmark & `cot=0' & `cot=128' & `cot=1024' \\
\midrule
7B & WikiContradict conflict & $0.2923$ & $0.4825$ & $0.4429$ \\
7B & ConflictBank conflict & $0.5505$ & $0.7531$ & $0.5308$ \\
14B & WikiContradict conflict & $0.2717$ & $0.4516$ & $0.3750$ \\
14B & ConflictBank conflict & $0.5876$ & $0.9449$ & $0.9513$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:theorem3} is the key theorem-3 result. The original monotone
story is too simple. On the 7B model, both conflict benchmarks show an
intermediate-CoT peak, with gap increases of $+0.1902$ on WikiContradict and
$+0.2026$ on ConflictBank from `cot=0' to `cot=128', followed by partial
recovery at `cot=1024'. On the 14B model, WikiContradict replicates the same
peak-and-recover shape, but ConflictBank conflict remains catastrophic through
long CoT: the gap rises from $0.5876$ to $0.9449$ and then stays at $0.9513$.

This is a better result than the dead monotone theorem, but the corrected
closed-book control also changes the story. Reasoning-time calibration failure
is \emph{structured}, not cleanly conflict-only. Medium CoT is often a
peak-overconfidence regime, but the late-stage behavior depends on benchmark
family and model scale. Naturalistic contradiction shows partial
self-correction; controlled conflict at 14B can stay locked into near-total
overconfidence.

The live same-family Qwen sweep already reinforces this split even before the
jobs finish. On partial Qwen-2.5-7B rows, ConflictBank conflict is already
near-maximally overconfident across all three CoT budgets
($0.9839 \rightarrow 0.9818 \rightarrow 0.9674$), while the corresponding
no-conflict slice stays small and even improves slightly
($0.0982 \rightarrow 0.0725 \rightarrow 0.0628$). On the partial
Qwen-2.5-32B WikiContradict slice, the gap grows from $0.1139$ to $0.3636$ at
medium CoT and then falls to $0.2486$ at long CoT, which is the first
same-family evidence for the rewritten two-regime self-correction story.

The true closed-book controls rule out a clean conflict-versus-no-conflict sign
flip. On ConflictBank closed-book, the 14B model already shows a strong gap
trajectory ($0.4917 \rightarrow 0.8168 \rightarrow 0.7890$), but explicit
conflict is worse still ($0.5876 \rightarrow 0.9449 \rightarrow 0.9513$). On
WikiContradict, the closed-book control is actually more overconfident than the
conflict condition for both models. So theorem 3 should be written as a
hard-QA overconfidence amplification result with a conflict-sensitive
interaction on controlled conflict families, not as a universal
conflict-conditioned sign flip.

\paragraph{Takeaways.}
The paper's empirical story is now coherent. Theorems 1 and 2 are supported by
corrected benchmark-backed regret results, including an expanded spotlight-scale
matrix. Theorem 3 lands in revised form on real reasoning traces and currently
rests on a completed DeepSeek 7B/14B family comparison plus an active same-family
Qwen 7B/14B/32B Delta sweep. What remains outside the current paper core are
the larger checkpoint-family sweeps and mitigation experiments such as bagged
reasoning or confidence-decoupled heads.
