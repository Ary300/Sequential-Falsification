\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Knowledge conflict benchmarks and analyses.}
Recent work has mapped the space of knowledge conflicts in LLMs into
context-memory, inter-context, and intra-memory forms
\citep{xu2024knowledgeconflicts}. ConflictQA and related studies show that
models are highly sensitive to coherent counter-memory and can exhibit
confirmation bias under multi-evidence conflict \citep{xie2024conflictqa}.
Large benchmark efforts such as ConflictBank, WikiContradict, and DynamicQA
provide the empirical substrate for studying controlled and naturalistic
conflicts \citep{su2024conflictbank,hou2024wikicontradict,marjanovic2024dynamicqa}.
These papers give the task surface, but not a Bayes-optimal or minimax account
of how arbitration should work.

\paragraph{Adaptive retrieval is mostly heuristic.}
PopQA-style adaptive retrieval uses popularity as a practical signal for when
retrieval helps, but the resulting policy is threshold-based rather than
decision-theoretically optimal \citep{mallen2023popqa}. Self-RAG and related
adaptive-RAG systems similarly learn or tune retrieval decisions without
deriving them from a posterior-predictive optimum \citep{asai2024selfrag}. Our
Theorems 1 and 2 aim at exactly this gap: a reliability-aware arbitration rule
and a formal statement that fixed trust policies are structurally dominated on
conflict families.

Closer decoder-level cousins such as CAD, AdaCAD, and CoCoA are also adaptive
but remain heuristic from our perspective. They implement context-sensitive
reweighting rules that are empirically strong, yet they do not derive the
weight from a posterior over source reliability. This is precisely where our
positioning matters. AdaCAD's token-level JSD weight can be read as a
first-order approximation to a Bayes-optimal arbitration weight
\citep{wang2025adacad}, while CoCoA combines entropy gap, contextual
peakedness, and divergence proxies into an effective retrieval-trust score
\citep{khandelwal2025cocoa}. In that sense, these baselines are not
counterexamples to our framing; they are evidence that the field has been
engineering approximations to the same object without writing down the
decision-theoretic target explicitly.

\paragraph{Mechanistic and calibration views of overthinking.}
Recent work shows that longer reasoning can hurt accuracy or calibration.
``Overthinking the Truth'' identifies a depth-wise failure mode in which models
move from correct intermediate beliefs to incorrect final ones under misleading
context \citep{halawi2024overthinking}. Chen et al.\ show that training-time
cross-entropy sharpening can harm pass@$N$, while Ghosal et al.\ document
non-monotone accuracy under large reasoning budgets \citep{chen2025rethinking,
ghosal2025mirage}. Sun et al.\ report that rationales and context reiteration
increase context reliance, especially on tasks where parametric knowledge
matters \citep{sun2025context}. Our calibration result is adjacent to this
literature but differs in two ways: it is conditioned on knowledge conflict,
and it is framed as a posterior misspecification phenomenon rather than a
purely empirical observation.

The closest empirical tension comes from recent calibration papers on reasoning
models. Yoon et al.\ find that reasoning models often express their confidence
more accurately on standard QA without explicit knowledge conflict
\citep{yoon2025confidence}. Lacombe et al.\ find the opposite trend on
knowledge-intensive confidence-assessment tasks, where larger reasoning budgets
hurt calibration \citep{lacombe2025dontthinktwice}. Welch et al.\ isolate
``implicit answer conditioning'' as a mechanism for CoT-induced overconfidence
in VLMs \citep{welch2026cost}, while Damani et al.\ show that binary reward
reasoning objectives can directly degrade calibration and that Brier-style
rewards repair it \citep{damani2025rlcr}. Our theorem-3 read is designed to
reconcile these observations: calibration can improve in well-specified or
low-conflict regimes and degrade in misspecified, endogenous-evidence regimes.

\paragraph{Misspecified Bayes as a template.}
The theorem-3 mechanism is best understood through classical results on
misspecified Bayesian updating. Berk established that under misspecification the
posterior concentrates on a KL projection rather than the truth
\citep{berk1966}, while Kleijn and van der Vaart made that concentration
picture explicit for misspecified posteriors \citep{kleijn2006}. Generalized
Bayes gives the right formal language for reasoning-time updates with an
effective learning rate $\eta$ \citep{bissiri2016}, and Grünwald--van Ommen
show how too-aggressive updates can produce hypercompression and
overconfidence-like failures under misspecification \citep{grunwald2017}. We
then need an endogenous-evidence account, because CoT traces are produced by
the same model whose beliefs are being updated; Berk--Nash style results supply
that bridge \citep{esponda2016,fudenberg2021}. Our contribution is not to
reprove those results, but to transport them into a reasoning-time arbitration
setting where the evidence is endogenous and the effective update rate differs
across conflict families.
