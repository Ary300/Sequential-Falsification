\section{Introduction}

Test-time scaling improves language-model performance by allocating additional inference-time compute through repeated sampling, reranking, or sequential refinement. In code generation and mathematical reasoning, the strongest gains often come from generating multiple candidates and selecting among them \citep{wang2022selfconsistency, chen2023selfdebug, li2025sstar}. However, repeated sampling is bottlenecked by verifier quality. Weak unit tests or answer-only grading can let incorrect candidates survive long enough to win a vote, even when more samples are generated. Recent analysis makes this failure mode explicit: with imperfect verifiers, resampling alone can hit a ceiling because false positives do not vanish with larger sample budgets \citep{stroebl2024flaws}.

This paper studies a different use of test-time compute. Instead of spending all extra budget on generation, we spend part of it on \emph{falsification}. We first sample a pool of candidate solutions. We then challenge each candidate with multiple targeted probes, such as held-out tests, simple mutations, or benchmark-specific execution checks, and eliminate candidates that fail. Surviving candidates are ranked by an empirical confidence proxy derived from the falsification trace. The high-level idea is simple: if false positives are the bottleneck, additional compute should be used to disprove wrong answers rather than merely produce more of them.

Our contribution is primarily systems and methodology rather than a finished benchmark sweep. We build a unified evaluation stack for HumanEval+, MBPP+, LiveCodeBench, and MATH-style problems, including corrected support for both function-completion tasks and stdin/stdout competitive-programming tasks. The key novelty claim is intentionally narrow: we study \emph{execution-grounded sequential falsification for code generation} with population-level elimination, rather than claiming to be the first statistically grounded or calibration-aware test-time-scaling method in general. We also formalize a clean survival-bound perspective: under mild assumptions on per-round detection probability, the probability that an incorrect candidate survives all rounds decays multiplicatively with the number of probes. This gives a principled rationale for sequential elimination even before full-scale confidence calibration is solved.

The scientific claim we make is deliberately narrower than the aspirational project framing. The current implementation is \emph{inspired} by e-values and anytime-valid inference \citep{vovk2021evalues, ramdas2023savi, howard2021confidence}, but it does not yet calibrate false-alarm rates strongly enough to justify a formal anytime-valid guarantee for the final confidence score. This conservative framing is increasingly important because newer work has already explored conformal or calibration-oriented test-time scaling outside our exact setting. Accordingly, we treat the current confidence mechanism as an empirical ranking signal and evaluate its behavior through calibration diagnostics rather than theorem-level validity claims.

Across the current corrected DeltaAI paper runs, sequential falsification consistently improves over majority vote on every benchmark/model slice we report, with especially large gains on HumanEval+ and clear gains on MBPP+, LiveCodeBench, and math. The strongest current HumanEval+ row is DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B, where majority vote reaches $34.5 \pm 0.3$, falsification reaches $79.6 \pm 1.5$, and the oracle reaches $83.5 \pm 0.6$. At the same time, the comparison to self-debugging and stronger proxy baselines is genuinely nontrivial: self-debug currently matches or slightly exceeds falsification on several monitored rows, and some generated-test-filter or proxy rows are also competitive. We therefore frame the present contribution carefully. The pipeline now demonstrates that agreement-based selection can fail badly on reasoning-oriented code models, that active execution-grounded elimination can recover most of the oracle gap, and that the repository supports a credible calibration-oriented alternative to pure resampling. Whether falsification can become a robust accuracy win over mature self-debugging across the full benchmark matrix remains an open empirical question.
