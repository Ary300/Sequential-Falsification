\section{Appendix}

\subsection{Proof Sketch for Theorem 1}

Under a strictly proper scoring rule, the Bayes action is the posterior
predictive distribution. Once the latent reliability variable $r$ is introduced
and the answer posterior is parameterized as a reliability-aware interpolation
between parametric and contextual beliefs, the optimal action is the integral
over posterior reliability:
\[
p^\star(y \mid q,c)=\int p(y \mid q,c,r)\,p(r \mid q,c)\,dr.
\]
Restricting to the implemented log-linear family yields the decoder used by the
Bayes proxy experiments. The missing proof step in the current draft is the
identifiability lemma tying posterior reliability to observable signals.

\subsection{Proof Sketch for Theorem 2}

Take two conflict families, $D_+$ and $D_-$, such that context is respectively
reliable and misleading. Any fixed policy must fail on at least one of these
families. A Yao-style mixture over $\{D_+,D_-\}$ therefore induces a constant
lower bound on the best fixed policy. An adaptive policy that sees a separating
signal $s(q,c)$ incurs regret proportional to the signal error. This establishes
that fixed trust policies are minimax-suboptimal over the conflict family.

\subsection{Theorem-3 Reporting Convention}

The theorem-3 tables report the confidence--accuracy gap rather than only ECE.
For the conflict slices considered here, the gap is the cleanest expression of
the phenomenon of interest: confidence can rise sharply even when accuracy is
flat or falling. We still report ECE in the generated artifacts, and in some
slices the two are numerically very close.
