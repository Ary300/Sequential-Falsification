\section{Appendix}

\subsection{Proof Sketch for Theorem 1}

Under a strictly proper scoring rule, the Bayes action is the posterior
predictive distribution. Once the latent reliability variable $r$ is introduced
and the answer posterior is parameterized as a reliability-aware interpolation
between parametric and contextual beliefs, the optimal action is the integral
over posterior reliability:
\[
p^\star(y \mid q,c)=\int p(y \mid q,c,r)\,p(r \mid q,c)\,dr.
\]
Restricting to the implemented log-linear family yields the decoder used by the
Bayes proxy experiments. The missing proof step in the current draft is the
identifiability lemma tying posterior reliability to observable signals.

\subsection{Proof Sketch for Theorem 2}

Take two conflict families, $D_+$ and $D_-$, such that context is respectively
reliable and misleading. Any fixed policy must fail on at least one of these
families. A Yao-style mixture over $\{D_+,D_-\}$ therefore induces a constant
lower bound on the best fixed policy. An adaptive policy that sees a separating
signal $s(q,c)$ incurs regret proportional to the signal error. This establishes
that fixed trust policies are minimax-suboptimal over the conflict family.

\subsection{Theorem-3 Reporting Convention}

The theorem-3 tables report the confidence--accuracy gap rather than only ECE.
For the conflict slices considered here, the gap is the cleanest expression of
the phenomenon of interest: confidence can rise sharply even when accuracy is
flat or falling. We still report ECE in the generated artifacts, and in some
slices the two are numerically very close.

\subsection{Current 14B Scaling Snapshot}

The finished DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B run contains `4200` rows. Its
headline conflict-slice numbers are:

\begin{itemize}
\item WikiContradict conflict: $0.2717 \rightarrow 0.4516 \rightarrow 0.3750$
\item ConflictBank conflict: $0.5876 \rightarrow 0.9449 \rightarrow 0.9513$
\end{itemize}

The first row reinforces the intermediate-CoT peak. The second shows that some

is why the final theorem-3 statement is family-dependent rather than uniformly
non-monotone.

\subsection{Eta-Tempered Decoding Recipe}

The concrete intervention implied by theorem 3 is confidence-only
$\eta$-tempering of the post-trace answer distribution. The recommended recipe
is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item hold out a small calibration split for each model-and-benchmark cell;
\item generate standard CoT traces once;
\item sweep $\eta \in [0.1, 1.0]$ over the post-trace answer distribution;
\item choose the largest $\eta$ that improves Brier score without harming
accuracy on the calibration split;
\item treat large residual gaps after tempering as answer-level failures rather
than calibration-only failures.
\end{enumerate}

The finished 14B intervention read gives the operational headline directly:
conflict slices tolerate only about $0.52\times$ the do-no-harm $\eta$ of
no-conflict slices. WikiContradict conflict is almost fully recalibratable by
confidence-only tempering, while ConflictBank conflict is not.

\subsection{Benchmark-Family Consistency Read}

The overall spotlight-matrix bootstrap interval is the main uncertainty
statement in the paper, but the per-benchmark family breakdown is also
informative. On the expanded spotlight matrix, ConflictBank, FaithEval,
MemoTrap, and NQ-Swap are unanimous $5/5$ Bayes-over-heuristic wins across
model families, while PopQA is the lone mixed family because of the Llama-70B
outlier slice. On the theorem-3 proxy matrix, AmbigDocs, ConflictBank,
FaithEval, and RAMDocs are unanimous $5/5$ Bayes-over-heuristic wins, while
WikiContradict is a unanimous negative exception. This is the cleanest compact
summary of why theorem 3 should be written as benchmark-dependent rather than
universally positive at the regret layer.
