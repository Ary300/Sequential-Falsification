\title{When Should LLMs Trust Retrieval? Bayes-Optimal Knowledge Arbitration under Conflict}
\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Large language models routinely face knowledge conflict: parametric memory,
retrieved context, and longer reasoning traces can each support different
answers. Existing systems handle this with heuristics such as fixed trust in
retrieval, fixed trust in the base model, or hand-tuned adaptive rules, but the
field still lacks a decision-theoretic account of when each source should be
trusted. We formulate knowledge arbitration as a posterior-predictive decision
problem and derive a Bayes-style reliability-aware arbitration rule. On a
corrected broad benchmark wave spanning PopQA, DynamicQA, NQ-Swap, and
WikiContradict, the Bayes proxy achieves mean regret $-0.0461$ versus
$-0.0233$ for a generic adaptive heuristic, while fixed trust policies degrade
to $7.2237$ (`always\_context') and $5.9356$ (`always\_parametric'). On a
conflict-heavy wave, the gap widens to $-0.1256$ versus $-0.0752$, with fixed
policies at $5.9037$ and $7.1329$. On the expanded 5x5 spotlight matrix, the
Bayes proxy also beats the generic heuristic by $0.0833$ regret with a
bootstrap $95\%$ interval of $[0.0371, 0.1112]$. For reasoning-time
calibration, real DeepSeek-R1-Distill traces support a structurally motivated
misspecification claim rather than a closed-form theorem of equal rigor to the
first two results: the original monotone ``more chain of thought is worse''
story is too simple, and reasoning-time overconfidence under conflict depends
on conflict family and training family. On the 7B model, both WikiContradict
and ConflictBank show an intermediate-CoT peak followed by partial recovery; on
the 14B model, WikiContradict retains this peak-and-recover pattern, while
ConflictBank conflict stays catastrophically overconfident through long CoT. A
completed same-family Qwen sweep shows that this should be written as
benchmark-dependent two-regime behavior rather than a family-invariant
7B-vs-14B asymmetry. These results position knowledge arbitration as a
principled inference problem rather than a retrieval heuristic, and they show
that fixed trust policies fail badly enough to be a modeling error rather than
a tuning issue.
\end{abstract}
